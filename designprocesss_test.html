DESIGN PROCESS

_______________________________

The "Pipeline"

Design is a highly diverse and hard-to-pin-down discipline. You have to consider a myriad of factors that are often unique to the individual project (pacing, aesthetics, player feedback, difficulty, progression, goals, story, etc.). Because of this, there is no single "correct" way to tackle a problem. I have gained proficiency in a mechanics-first approach, utilizing the iterative method and rigorous playtesting to reach my design goals.

Ideation

I start my process with research - mostly by creating mind maps and gathering inspiring images and videos to associate the material with potential emotions, mechanics, and themes. In this phase, the freakier a source is, the more inspiring it can be. Inspiration can come from absolutely anything; often, the most emotive sources produce the wildest ideas, which frequently turn into the most engaging mechanics.

Prototyping

My intuition is usually a bad indicator of how good an idea actually is, so I build quick prototypes to test those concepts out! I make heavy use of GIF recordings and detailed note-taking to build out my documentation. I also rely on source control to seamlessly switch between variations, allowing me to try out different ideas without losing previous work.

Assessment

Playtesting and solo testing are an automatic part of my routine, but I always seek feedback from as many people as possible; there is always some oversight when designing in isolation. Watching people play, taking notes, and asking open-ended questions always yields surprising results and reveals things I didn't think of. I focus heavily on this early in development to ensure the project is heading in the right direction, which is when the most impactful changes can still be made.

habits and improvements

Some of the habits I have cultivated include keeping a journal for my personal and professinal projects. One of my biggest struggles with designing is tunnel vision. I'll often hyperfocus on a single mechanic or element in lieu of the whole experience. Though with colleagues I'm constantly talking through my process and so this can be resolved fairly quickly. A majour goal I've set for myself is learning to define good enough in the scope of a project. Re-evaluating myself often has been instrumental here.

Documentation

Documentation is an important part of keeping our process accountable and actionable. I make use of charts and diagrams to visualize mechanics and their interactions, while maintaining detailed lists of mechanics throughout the development process.

It's important when writing and maintaining documentation that the thought process behind the mechanics and systems is as unambiguous as possible. Therefore where possible I explain my thought process with either diagrams, math formula or snippets of code to explain my process.

Here is an exerpt from a Game Design Document that I wrote for an underwater vr game actually played underwater:

2.2 Mechanic: Player Controller

The player's movement is dictated by their head locomotion and a spline within the level. The player's position is fixed on a flat plane which exists perpendicularly to the spline and travels along the spline at a dynamic rate, dictated by rules outlined in the speed section.

________________________________________

Control

The player is afforded some degree of control in their position on this plane. In this case they have two head gestures which purpose movement along this plane. The player's speed in moving around is determined by the player's speed stat (part of the player controller).

They can tilt their head up and down. The tilt then moves the player directly up or down on this plane, again perpendicularly to the spline.


velocityUP = vector3.up * cos(tiltX) * speed

They are also able to tilt their head left and right. The tilt then moves the player directly left or right on this plane, still perpendicularly to the spline.



velocityX = vector3.right * cos(tiltZ) * speed

They are also freely able to rotate their heads along the Y axis to look around as in most other VR games. In other words, they can look from side to side without affecting their relative position on the plane. This is explicitly to allow the player to look around and enjoy the environment without being interrupted by the movement mechanic.


________________________________________

Speed

The player's speed along the spline is dynamic and can be affected by several factors including:

  1. Buffs: Element System: Buffs
  2. Obstacles: Element Mechanic: Obstacles
  3. Hazards: Element System: Hazards
  4. Enemies: Element System: Enemies

________________________________________

Speed lines

The player's speed is also indicated to them by speed lines which are shown at the border of their vision. The faster a player moves the more plentiful and stretched the speed lines will be. Speed lines will be generated with a particle system and a trail renderer.

parameters
  • Start showing: Any speed above base speed
  • Starting rate: 20
  • Rate at max speed: 50
  • Lifetime: 0.5s
Programming guidelines
  • OnStartSpeedLines
  • OnStopSpeedLines
  • OnSpeedChanged //adjust the rate of particles spawned
